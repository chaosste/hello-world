EntheoGen Manifesto

What is EntheoGen?

EntheoGen is an open, evolving harm-reduction database that maps interactions between entheogenic substances (ayahuasca, psilocybin, mescaline, DMT, and others) and medications, street drugs, and co-administered plant medicines. It is built for intentional, ceremonial, and psychonautic use patterns—not generic recreational summaries.

We center on contexts where these substances are actually used: Indigenous ceremonies, underground facilitator-led retreats, clinical trials, and solo psychonautic exploration. Our goal is to provide decision-useful, evidence-graded information to reduce preventable harm while respecting the complexity of psychedelic practice.

Why does EntheoGen exist?

The problem:

Existing drug interaction resources are built for clinical pharmacy contexts. They either:

  • Ignore entheogenic substances entirely, or
  • Provide overly cautious blanket warnings without practical guidance, or
  • Focus on "abuse" framing that alienates ceremonial and intentional users.

The gap:

People making real-world decisions—facilitators screening ceremony participants, therapists integrating clients on medications, informed users navigating polypharmacy—lack a single, transparent, evidence-graded reference that:

  • Distinguishes "blunted effects" from "life-threatening toxicity."
  • Provides timing and washout guidance for sequenced multi-medicine work.
  • Separates strong formal evidence from mechanistic inference from underground consensus.
  • Acknowledges when we simply don't know.

Our response:

EntheoGen organizes what is known, marks what is uncertain, and makes it accessible to those who need it most.

What EntheoGen is NOT

1. Medical advice

EntheoGen does not diagnose, treat, or prescribe. It is an information tool, not a substitute for clinical judgment or medical supervision. Users remain responsible for their own decisions and should consult qualified professionals where appropriate.

2. A recommendation to use psychedelics

We do not advocate for or against use of any substance. EntheoGen exists to reduce harm for those who have already chosen to engage with these substances, regardless of legal status.

3. A static, finished reference

This is a living dataset. Psychedelic science is rapidly evolving; underground practice continues to surface new patterns; Indigenous knowledge systems offer perspectives often absent from Western literature. EntheoGen will be updated as new evidence emerges, and we will version and document all changes.

4. A single epistemology

We do not privilege peer-reviewed literature over all other knowledge. EntheoGen integrates:

  • Formal evidence (clinical trials, case reports, toxicology reviews)
  • Mechanistic inference (pharmacology, receptor binding, metabolism)
  • Field consensus (facilitator protocols, harm-reduction organizations, ethnographic observation)
  • Recurrent anecdotal patterns (user reports, community forums)

Each source type is explicitly labeled with evidence tiers so users can weigh them according to their own risk tolerance and context.

5. A replacement for Indigenous expertise

Where traditional knowledge exists, we will cite and defer to it—but we will not appropriate or decontextualize it. EntheoGen is a complement to, not a substitute for, guidance of trained curanderos, facilitators, and Elders.

Core principles

1. Transparency over authority

We show our sources, mark our uncertainties, and version our changes. If we don't know, we say so explicitly. Users should be able to trace every classification back to its evidence base.

2. Safety through specificity

Generic warnings ("contraindicated") without mechanism or context create fear and non-compliance. We provide:

  • Mechanism summaries (why is this risky?)
  • Primary risk types (cardiovascular? psychiatric? blunted effects?)
  • Timing guidance (can these be sequenced safely? how much washout?)
  • Context notes (different risk in ceremonial vs clinical vs solo settings)

This allows users to make informed, context-appropriate decisions rather than blanket avoidance.

3. Respect for multiple use contexts

A microdosing stack managed by a psychiatrist is different from a multi-day Shipibo retreat. A clinical psilocybin trial under medical supervision is different from solo mushroom work in nature. EntheoGen models context as a first-class variable, not an afterthought.

4. Bias toward caution when uncertain

When evidence is ambiguous or absent, we classify interactions conservatively. Better to over-caution and update later than to under-warn and miss a serious risk.

5. Iterative improvement through community

EntheoGen is strengthened by expertise of its users. We welcome contributions from:

  • Researchers and clinicians
  • Facilitators and integration therapists
  • Experienced psychonauts
  • Indigenous knowledge-holders (with appropriate permission and co-authorship)

We will build tools for expert elicitation and collaborative review, treating dataset as a collective knowledge commons.

What we believe about harm reduction

  • Abstinence-only messaging fails. People will continue to use psychedelics regardless of legal status or clinical availability. Our role is to make that use as safe as possible.
  • Information access saves lives. Withholding or obscuring interaction data on moral or legal grounds increases preventable harm. Transparency and accessibility are ethical imperatives.
  • Uncertainty is not a reason to stay silent. Even when formal evidence is limited, we can share what underground practice, mechanistic reasoning, and traditional knowledge suggest—as long as we label it clearly.
  • Blunted effects matter as much as toxicity. A person on an SSRI who wastes money and emotional energy on a "failed" psilocybin ceremony because no one told them about serotonin-receptor occupancy has been harmed, even if not medically. We address both safety and effectiveness.
  • Context shapes risk. The same substance pair may be low-risk in a clinical setting with monitoring and high-risk in an unsupervised ceremonial context. EntheoGen models this explicitly.

Scope and limitations

In scope:

  • Substance–substance interactions for classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT/ayahuasca, mescaline, 5-MeO-DMT), dissociatives (ketamine, salvia), empathogens (MDMA), and deliriants (tropane alkaloids) with:
    • Psychiatric medications (SSRIs, SNRIs, antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, benzodiazepines)
    • Cardiovascular medications (antihypertensives, beta-blockers, etc.)
    • Other entheogens and co-administered plant medicines (kambo, rapé, cannabis, yopo, bufo)
    • Street drugs (stimulants, opioids, alcohol)
  • Context variables: ceremonial, clinical, naturalistic, microdosing
  • Timing and washout guidance for sequenced use
  • Evidence tier classification (1 = strong formal, 2 = mechanistic, 3 = field consensus, 4 = anecdotal)

Future scope (not yet implemented):

  • Set and setting modifiers: How ceremony structure, music, sitter presence, intention-setting, and integration practices influence outcomes and risk
  • Individual risk factors: Age, cardiovascular disease, psychiatric history, genetic polymorphisms (CYP enzymes), pregnancy/lactation
  • Dosage and preparation variations: How brew strength, mushroom species, synthetic vs plant-derived, and administration route affect interactions

Out of scope:

  • Legal advice: EntheoGen does not comment on the legality of any substance or practice
  • Therapeutic protocols: We do not prescribe dosing regimens or treatment plans
  • Sourcing or preparation: We do not provide information on obtaining, cultivating, or preparing substances
  • Diagnosis: We do not assess whether a given person should or should not use psychedelics

Who is EntheoGen for?

Primary users:

  • Facilitators and ceremony leaders screening participants and managing multi-medicine retreats
  • Integration therapists and psychedelic-assisted therapists working with clients on psychiatric medications
  • Clinical researchers designing trials and safety protocols
  • Informed psychonauts navigating their own medication regimens or polypharmacy decisions
  • Harm-reduction organizations seeking evidence-graded references for training and client support

Secondary users:

  • Medical professionals (GPs, psychiatrists, emergency physicians) encountering patients who use psychedelics
  • Ethnobotanists and anthropologists documenting traditional and contemporary use patterns
  • Policy researchers seeking data on real-world interaction risks

How EntheoGen is built

Data sources (in priority order):

  1. Peer-reviewed literature: Clinical trials, case reports, systematic reviews, toxicology studies, pharmacokinetic research
  2. Clinical pharmacology references: Drug interaction databases, MAOI guidance, receptor binding profiles
  3. Harm-reduction organizations: ICEERS, DanceSafe, Zendo Project, PsychonautWiki, Erowid, Bluelight
  4. Ethnographic and traditional knowledge: Published anthropological work, facilitator protocols, Indigenous guidance (with permission)
  5. User reports: Aggregated patterns from trip reports and community forums (clearly labeled as lowest-evidence tier)

Quality standards:

  • Every interaction entry includes:
    • Classification (low risk / caution / unsafe / contraindicated / unknown)
    • Evidence tiers (1–4)
    • Mechanism summary
    • Primary risk types
    • Timing/washout guidance where relevant
    • Contextual notes
  • Changes are versioned and documented in Git
  • Conflicts or ambiguities are flagged for expert review
  • We admit uncertainty explicitly rather than inventing data

Governance:

  • Data curation team reviews all entries for accuracy and consistency
  • Ethics and academic oversight ensures compliance with research standards
  • Community input via expert elicitation, beta testing, and public comment (post-beta)
  • Open licensing (CC BY-SA or similar) to maximize accessibility while requiring attribution and share-alike for derivatives

What success looks like

  • A facilitator uses EntheoGen to safely allow a participant on lamotrigine to attend ceremony, where they would have been excluded under blanket "no psych meds" rules.
  • A therapist helps a client taper mirtazapine two weeks before a psilocybin session, restoring psychedelic responsivity and avoiding a "failed" experience.
  • A researcher cites EntheoGen's interaction dataset in a clinical trial protocol, improving participant screening and safety monitoring.
  • An emergency physician consults EntheoGen when a patient presents with serotonin toxicity after ayahuasca + polypharmacy, guiding appropriate benzodiazepine use.
  • A solo psychonaut avoids a dangerous ayahuasca + yopo combination because they understand mechanistic risk and absence of Indigenous precedent, explicitly marked in database.
  • A harm-reduction organization integrates EntheoGen into training materials, replacing vague warnings with actionable, evidence-graded guidance.
  • A clinical journal publishes EntheoGen's methodology and dataset as a model for structured expert elicitation in under-researched areas.

Invitation to collaborate

EntheoGen is stronger with diverse expertise. We welcome contributions from:

  • Academic researchers (pharmacology, toxicology, psychiatry, anthropology)
  • Clinicians and therapists (psychiatry, emergency medicine, addiction medicine)
  • Facilitators and ceremony leaders (ayahuasca, psilocybin, peyote, iboga, San Pedro)
  • Harm-reduction practitioners (DanceSafe, Zendo, festival medics)
  • Indigenous knowledge-holders (with appropriate consent, co-authorship, and compensation)
  • Experienced psychonauts willing to share observational patterns (anonymously)

If you see an error, identify a gap, or have evidence we've missed, we want to hear from you.

Acknowledgment of limitations

We are building this tool with limited resources, in a legally and ethically complex space, on a timeline measured in months rather than decades. EntheoGen will be incomplete, imperfect, and evolving. We accept this.

Our responsibility is not to be perfect. It is to be:

  • Transparent about what we know and don't know
  • Rigorous in our methods and evidence standards
  • Responsive to feedback and new data
  • Accountable to communities who rely on this information
